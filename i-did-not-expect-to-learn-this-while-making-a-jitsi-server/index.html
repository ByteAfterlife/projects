I did not expect to learn this while making a Jitsi server

Byte December 22, 2025

In my last post of the Jitsi server series, I setup client certificate authentication to log people in securely into my server if they're on mobile data and they cant be authorized by their IP (because its constantly changing). Same day yesterday, I tried making a client certificate for one of my family members, as I did, it said "Certificate password is incorrect" when I tried importing the PFX on their system, and I was super confused, I even tried verifying the password myself with openssl, and it worked. Today, I tried making one for another family member, and on their phone, same thing, "Enter correct password" it said. I was so confused. Why? The password was correct. I even tried empty passwords (yes, you can make pfx's with empty passwords if you didnt know) AND IT STILL SAID WRONG. YOU CANNOT MAKE A TYPO ON A EMPTY PASSWORD. I knew I'm not going crazy.. right? They typed it correctly, I tried an empty one, they didnt type anything, and it still said its wrong. WHYYYY. I already knew its probably something with compatibility, my system is very up to date, theirs.. well I wont say. Anyway, I WAS RIGHT. 2 hours ago, I discovered the encryption algorithm that my certificate creator was using by default was not compatible with older devices. I then bragged to myself about how I knew it was something with compatibility because I had to let it out. Anyway, then after some extra parameters in the final command to bundle the key and certificate into a cute pfx, it actually imported successfully. See? I knew I wasn't going crazy. I KNEW ITTTTT. Anyway, now I learned that pfx's have different algorithms for.. encrypting themselves. Damn like just stick to one. In my family members' older devices' defense, it was saying the password was wrong because it couldn't decrypt the keys and certificates with the password because it was using a more secure and modern encryption algorithm that they have not ever seen in their lives.
